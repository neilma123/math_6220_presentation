Bayesian Approach to Hurst Exponent Estimation - Dlask et. al

Introduction

  • Last time we talked about simulating fBM given a Hurst exponent; today, we do the reverse: given time series data, we estimate the Hurst exponent.
  • There are several traditional estimators for the Hurst exponent: zero-crossings, quadratic variations, rescaled range (R/S), and the Whittle estimator. Each has strengths and limitations, particularly that most provide only point estimates, without uncertainty bounds or full sampling distributions.
  • Bayesian estimation, in contrast, produces uncertainty bounds and sampling distributions, offering richer interpretability.

Traditional Methods

Fractional Gaussian Noise and Its Connection to fBM

  • Fractional Brownian Motion (fBM):
    A continuous-time Gaussian process \(B_H(t)\) with parameter \(H \in (0,1)\) (the Hurst exponent), defined by: \[ \mathbb{E}[B_H(t)] = 0 \] \[ \text{Cov}(B_H(t), B_H(s)) = \frac{1}{2} \left( |t|^{2H} + |s|^{2H} - |t-s|^{2H} \right) \]

  • Fractional Gaussian Noise (fGn):
    The stationary increment process of fBM, defined as \[ x_k(H) = B_H(k+1) - B_H(k) \] for \(k = 1, \dots, N\).

  • fGn is a zero-mean, stationary Gaussian process characterized by \(H\) and is often used as a model of fractal or long-range-dependent noise in time series.

Zero-Crossings

Fractional Gaussian Noise and Autocorrelation

The autocorrelation function for the fGn is \[ \rho_k = \frac{1}{2} \left( |k+1|^{2H} - 2|k|^{2H} + |k-1|^{2H} \right) \]

In particular: \[ \rho \equiv \rho_1 = 2^{2H-1} - 1 \]

Probability of Zero-Crossing

For two consecutive samples \((x_k, x_{k+1})\), the joint density of the bivariate normal vector with correlation \(\rho\) is: \[ f(x_k, x_{k+1}) = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \exp \left( -\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)} (x_k^2 - 2\rho x_k x_{k+1} + x_{k+1}^2) \right) \]

The zero-crossing probability is: \[ p^* = \Pr(x_k x_{k+1} < 0) \]

By variable transformation and polar coordinates, one obtains: \[ p^* = \frac{1}{\pi} \text{arccot}\left( \frac{\rho}{\sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \right ) \]

Using \(\rho = 2^{2H-1}-1\), the point estimator for \(H\) is: \[ H = 1 + \log_2 \left( \cos \frac{\pi p^*}{2} \right ) \]

Empirically, \(p^*\) is approximated by the relative number of zero-crossings \(Z/N\).

Quadratic Variations

Mean Square Increment of Fractional Brownian Motion

Let \(\{B_H(t)\}\) be a fractional Brownian motion with Hurst exponent \(H \in (0,1)\). The covariance function is \[ \mathbb{E}[B_H(t)B_H(s)] = \frac{1}{2}\left( |t|^{2H} + |s|^{2H} - |t-s|^{2H} \right). \]

The mean square increment over lag \(h\) is: \[ \mathbb{E}\left[ \left(B_H(t+h) - B_H(t)\right)^2 \right] = \mathbb{E}[B_H(t+h)^2] + \mathbb{E}[B_H(t)^2] - 2\mathbb{E}[B_H(t+h)B_H(t)]. \]

Plugging in, since \(\mathbb{E}[B_H(t)^2] = |t|^{2H}\) and \(\mathbb{E}[B_H(t+h)B_H(t)] = \frac{1}{2}\left( |t+h|^{2H} + |t|^{2H} - |h|^{2H} \right)\), we obtain: \[ \begin{align*} \mathbb{E}\left[ \left(B_H(t+h) - B_H(t)\right)^2 \right] &= |t+h|^{2H} + |t|^{2H} - \left( |t+h|^{2H} + |t|^{2H} - |h|^{2H} \right) \\ &= |h|^{2H} \end{align*} \]

Thus, for all \(t\), \[ \mathbb{E}\left[ \left(B_H(t+h) - B_H(t)\right)^2 \right] = h^{2H} \]

For observed time series \(\{X_t\}\):

  • Compute the empirical quadratic variation at lag \(h\): \[ V(h) = \frac{1}{N-h} \sum_{t=1}^{N-h} (X_{t+h} - X_t)^2 \]
  • For fractional Brownian motion, \(\mathbb{E}[V(h)] \propto h^{2H}\).
  • Estimate \(H\) by linear regression of \(\log V(h)\) against \(\log h\): \[ \log V(h) \approx c + 2H \log h \] so \(H \approx \frac{1}{2} \frac{d}{d\log h}\log V(h)\).

Rescaled Range (R/S) Method

Scaling Properties of the Range of Cumulative Deviations

Given a time series \(\{X_t\}\) of length \(N\), consider its partial sums (“cumulative deviations from the mean”):

\[ Y_t = \sum_{i=1}^t (X_i - \bar{X}_n), \qquad t=1,\ldots,n \]

where \(\bar{X}_n\) is the mean of the segment of length \(n\).

  • The range \(R(n)\) of these cumulative deviations is: \[ R(n) = \max_{1 \leq t \leq n} Y_t - \min_{1 \leq t \leq n} Y_t \]
  • For a self-similar process like fractional Brownian motion (fBM) with Hurst exponent \(H\), \(R(n)\) grows as a power law with the segment size: \[ \mathbb{E}[R(n)] \propto n^H \]

To compare signals with different variances, the range is normalized by the standard deviation \(S(n)\): \[ Q(n) = \frac{R(n)}{S(n)} \] where \[ S(n) = \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^n (X_t - \bar{X}_n)^2 \right)^{1/2} \]

For self-similar processes, \[ \mathbb{E}[Q(n)] \propto n^H \] Therefore, if you plot \(\log Q(n)\) against \(\log n\), the slope gives an estimate of the Hurst exponent \(H\).

This estimator was introduced by Hurst:

Given a time series \(\{X_t\}\) of length \(N\):

  • For subintervals of length \(n\):
    1. Compute mean: \(\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n} X_t\).
    2. Form cumulative deviations: \(Y_t = \sum_{i=1}^t (X_i - \bar{X}_n)\).
    3. Compute the range: \(R(n) = \max_{1 \leq t \leq n} Y_t - \min_{1 \leq t \leq n} Y_t\).
    4. Compute the standard deviation: \(S(n) = \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^n (X_t - \bar{X}_n)^2 \right)^{1/2}\).
    5. Compute the rescaled range: \(Q(n) = R(n)/S(n)\).
  • For self-similar processes, \(\mathbb{E}[Q(n)] \propto n^H\).
  • Estimate \(H\) by linear regression of \(\log Q(n)\) vs. \(\log n\): \[ \log Q(n) \approx c + H \log n \]

Whittle Estimator

  • Spectral Density: The spectral density \(f_H(\lambda)\) of fractional Gaussian noise (fGn) — and more generally of stationary long-memory processes — behaves, for small frequencies \(\lambda\), as: \[ f_H(\lambda) \propto |\lambda|^{-(2H-1)} \]
  • Periodogram as Approximate Sufficient Statistic: For large sample size \(N\), the periodogram ordinates \(I(\lambda_j)\) at Fourier frequencies \(\lambda_j\) are (approximately) independent and follow an exponential distribution with mean \(f_H(\lambda_j)\).
  • Whittle Likelihood: Using the (approximate) independence and exponential distribution of \(I(\lambda_j)\), we can write an approximate likelihood for \(H\) (and variance), allowing efficient inference even in long-memory models.

Derivation of the Spectral Density for fGn

The autocovariance function of fGn is \[ \gamma(k) = \frac{1}{2} \left(|k+1|^{2H} - 2|k|^{2H} + |k-1|^{2H}\right) \]

The spectral density is the Fourier transform of the autocovariance: \[ f_H(\lambda) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \gamma(k) e^{-i k \lambda} \] For small \(|\lambda|\), the sum can be approximated and yields: \[ f_H(\lambda) \propto |\lambda|^{-(2H-1)}, \quad \lambda \to 0 \]

This power-law behavior at low frequencies reflects the long-range dependence when \(H > 0.5\).

Why Are Periodogram Ordinates Approximately Independent and Exponentially Distributed?

Let \(\{X_t\}\) be a zero-mean stationary Gaussian time series of length \(N\). The (normalized) discrete Fourier transform (DFT) is \[ \tilde{X}(\lambda_j) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{t=1}^N X_t e^{-i \lambda_j t}, \quad \lambda_j = \frac{2\pi j}{N}, \quad j=1,\dots, \Big\lfloor \frac{N}{2} \Big\rfloor \] The periodogram at frequency \(\lambda_j\) is \(I(\lambda_j) = |\tilde{X}(\lambda_j)|^2\)

  • For large \(N\), by the central limit theorem and properties of the Fourier basis, \(\tilde{X}(\lambda_j)\) becomes (approximately) a complex normal random variable, with \[ \mathbb{E}\left[\,|\tilde{X}(\lambda_j)|^2\,\right] = f_H(\lambda_j) \]
  • If \(\tilde{X}(\lambda_j) \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, f_H(\lambda_j))\) (complex normal with mean zero, variance \(f_H(\lambda_j)\)), then \(I(\lambda_j)\) (the squared modulus) is exponentially distributed: \[ I(\lambda_j) \sim \text{Exp}\Big(\frac{1}{f_H(\lambda_j)}\Big) \] with mean \(f_H(\lambda_j)\).
  • For large \(N\), DFT coefficients at different \(\lambda_j\) are nearly uncorrelated (orthogonality of the basis), so \(I(\lambda_j)\) are approximately independent.

The Whittle estimator is a semi-parametric maximum likelihood approach leveraging the spectral density of the process.

  • The spectrum at frequency \(f\) behaves as \(S(f) \propto |f|^{-(2H-1)}\) at low frequencies.
  • The Whittle likelihood is \[ L(H, \sigma^2) = \prod_{j=1}^N \frac{1}{f_H(\lambda_j)} \exp\left(-\frac{I(\lambda_j)}{f_H(\lambda_j)}\right) \] where \(I(\lambda_j)\) is the periodogram at frequency \(\lambda_j\) and \(f_H\) is the theoretical spectral density for given \(H\).
  • The estimator \(\hat{H}_{\text{Whittle}}\) is the value of \(H\) which maximizes the (approximate) log-likelihood: \[ \hat{H}_{\text{Whittle}} = \arg\max_H \log L(H, \sigma^2) \]
  • Often restricted to low-frequency bands for better consistency.

Bayesian Estimation

Prior for Zero-Crossing Probability

  • In the Bayesian approach, we treat the zero-crossing probability \(p^*\) as a random variable.
  • Prior for \(p^*\):
    We impose a Beta-type prior: \[ f_{\text{PRIOR}}(p^*) = \frac{(p^*)^{-a}(1-p^*)^{-a}}{B(1-a, 1-a)}, \quad a \in [0,1) \]
    • \(a = 0\): uniform prior (all \(p^*\) equally likely).
    • \(a = 1/2\): Jeffreys–Perks prior (reference prior for binomial).
  • The choice of \(a\) controls the informativeness of the prior.

Likelihood for Zero-Crossing Counts

  • Given a signal of length \(N+1\) and an observed number of zero-crossings \(Z\), the likelihood for \(p^*\) is binomial: \[ Z \mid p^* \sim \mathrm{Binomial}(N, p^*) \] \[ f(Z \mid p^*) = \binom{N}{Z} (p^*)^Z (1-p^*)^{N-Z} \]
  • Each crossing is considered a Bernoulli trial, with probability \(p^*\) that the sign changes between consecutive values.

Posterior for Zero-Crossing Probability

  • Posterior (using Bayes’ theorem): \[ f_{\text{POST}}(p^* \mid Z) = \frac{ (p^*)^{Z-a} (1-p^*)^{N-Z-a} }{ B(Z+1-a,\ N-Z+1-a) } \]
  • In other words, the posterior is a Beta distribution: \[ p^* \mid Z \sim \mathrm{Beta}(Z+1-a,\, N-Z+1-a) \]
  • This posterior for \(p^*\) is then mapped to a posterior for the Hurst exponent \(H\) via the transformation: \[ H \approx 1 + \log_2\left( \cos \frac{\pi p^*}{2} \right) \]
  • Thus, Bayesian inference yields a full distribution (not just a point estimate) for \(H\), meaning uncertainty can be rigorously quantified.

Signal Segmentation

Why Segment the Signal?

  • Real-world signals (EEG, finance, environmental data) often exhibit time-varying roughness and are not globally well-modeled by a single Hurst exponent.
  • Fractional Gaussian noise (fGn) is stationary only in the increments; empirical data may be only locally stationary.
  • To detect structural changes in persistence or anti-persistence, we divide the observed signal into non-overlapping segments, estimate local \(H\) in each, and analyze its behavior over time.

Segmentation Framework

  • Take a time series of length \(L(N+1)\).
  • Partition it into \(L\) disjoint segments, each containing \(N+1\) points: \[ \{x_1,\dots,x_{N+1}\},\quad \{x_{N+2},\dots,x_{2N+2}\},\dots \]
  • Let \(Z_k\) denote the number of zero-crossings in segment \(k\), \(k = 1,\dots,L\).

Because each segment is modeled as fGn with its own zero-crossing count, each segment contributes a segment-wise posterior for the zero-crossing probability \(p\): \[ p \mid Z_k \sim \mathrm{Beta}(Z_k+1-a,\, N-Z_k+1-a). \]

The Aggregate Posterior for \(p\)

For the entire segmented signal, define the aggregate probability density: \[ f_L(p) = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{k=1}^{L} \frac{ p^{Z_k - a}\,(1-p)^{N - Z_k - a} }{ B(Z_k+1-a,\, N-Z_k+1-a) }. \]

Interpretation:

  • Each segment contributes a Beta-shaped posterior based on its own zero-crossing count.
  • \(f_L(p)\) is simply the mixture of segment posteriors.
  • When the signal has homogeneous roughness, these posteriors overlap and \(f_L(p)\) is sharply peaked.
  • When roughness varies, segment posteriors differ, and \(f_L(p)\) broadens or becomes multimodal.

From Aggregate Posterior to Posterior of \(H\)

Since \[ H = 1 + \log_2\left( \cos\frac{\pi p}{2} \right), \] and this mapping is monotone, the distribution of \(H\) follows immediately from \(f_L(p)\).

Mean: \[ E(H) = 1 + \int_0^1 f_L(p)\,\log_2\left(\cos\frac{\pi p}{2}\right)\,dp. \]

Variance: \[ \sigma^2(H) = \int_0^1 \left[1+\log_2\left(\cos\frac{\pi p}{2}\right)\right]^2 f_L(p)\,dp - [E(H)]^2. \]

These quantities summarize the overall roughness and variability across all segments.

Optimal Segmentation

Why Choose \(L\) Carefully?

  • If \(L\) is too small (segments too long): local structure is averaged out → missed regime changes.
  • If \(L\) is too large (segments too short): zero-crossing counts become noisy → posteriors broaden and become unstable.
  • We need a principled rule to select the optimal number of segments \(L^*\).

Axiom 1: Unimodality of \(f_L(p)\)

The aggregate posterior \(f_L(p)\) should be unimodal.

Rationale:

  • If the underlying process has approximately constant roughness within each segment, the individual segment posteriors should align in a common region.

  • Their mixture should then have a single dominant peak.

  • Multimodality typically signals:

    • insufficient data per segment,
    • excessive segmentation,
    • or large, unmodeled heterogeneity.

Thus unimodality ensures statistical coherence of the segmentation.

Axiom 2: Minimum Sufficient Segmentation

Among all \(L\) for which \(f_L(p)\) is unimodal, choose the smallest such \(L > 1\):

\[ L^* = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if $f_L(p)$ is unimodal for all tested segmentations} \\ \min_{L>1}\{L : f_L(p)\ \text{is unimodal}\}, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Interpretation:

  • We want the simplest segmentation that yields a stable, unimodal posterior.
  • In practice, this coincides with minimizing the posterior variance of \(H\).

Credible Interval Construction Under \(L^*\)

Given the optimal segmentation \(L^*\), compute boundaries \(p_{\min}, p_{\max}\) from: \[ \int_0^{p_{\min}} f_{L^*}(p)\,dp = \frac{\alpha}{2}, \qquad \int_0^{p_{\max}} f_{L^*}(p)\,dp = 1 - \frac{\alpha}{2}. \]

Transform via the monotone mapping to obtain a credible interval for \(H\): \[ H_{\min} = 1 + \log_2\left(\cos\frac{\pi p_{\max}}{2}\right), \qquad H_{\max} = 1 + \log_2\left(\cos\frac{\pi p_{\min}}{2}\right). \]

These are fully Bayesian uncertainty bounds capturing segmentation variability, binomial uncertainty, and transformation uncertainty.
